You'll find some sample use cases here, derived from real world scenarios. You can use them for inspiration.

You'll notice that the messages never include anything useful on its own if they were to fall in the wrong hands.
That's the intended purpose: data stored online should not be enough to derive your key.

Paper backup for someone who knows about crypto

  • The recipient knows about cryptocurrencies, and only needs the recovery passphrase.
Dear redacted,
If you receive this, I've been away for a long time.
You know I have some cryptocurrency, as we've discussed this in the past. You'll need the codes to access those. In a black fabric pouch in my nightstand, you'll find an orange paper with 24 words written in it. You can use those with any dedicated hardware (Trezor, or Ledger) to access the funds.
Love you,

Partial paper backup for someone who knows about crypto

  • The recipient knows about cryptocurrencies, and only needs the recovery passphrase.
  • For safety, four words are not written on the paper, and are instead sent in the message.
    • This is not a perfect failsafe. Someone finding the paper with the missing four words might still be able to bruteforce your wallet address.
    • However, someone simply finding the four words would be unable to do anything with them.
Dear redacted,
It's been good while it lasted... remember when we discussed Bitcoin? I've setup this message to be sent automatically after I pass away.
In our holiday home, behind the washing machine, there's a plastified sheet with 20 seemingly random words.
Here are the four missing ones: umbrella, hamster, you, easily. Add the missing ones in this order after the first 20, and you can use this to reseed a wallet. If you're not sure of what you're doing, you can contact Christopher B. for help, I trust him.
Please use the funds as you wish, and don't miss me too much, I'm gone already. Life is short, enjoy it.
